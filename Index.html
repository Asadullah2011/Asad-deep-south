Deep South

History

The Deep South primarily includes the states of Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, and South Carolina and has a history closely tied to slavery and plantation agriculture. In the 17th and 18th centuries, European colonial powers settled in the region, with slave labor being particularly important in cotton farming. During the Civil War (1861-1865), the Southern states seceded from the Union and formed the Confederate States of America. The war ended with the South’s defeat and the abolition of slavery. After the war, the federal government tried to reintegrate the South, but the introduction of "Jim Crow" laws led to racial segregation and discrimination. In the 1950s and 1960s, the Civil Rights Movement, led by figures like Martin Luther King Jr., fought for equality, resulting in the passing of laws like the Civil Rights Act (1964) and the Voting Rights Act (1965). Today, the Deep South is a culturally rich region that has become more economically diverse, but it still grapples with the legacy of segregation and inequality.
